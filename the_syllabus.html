The Progressive #32
May 9 - May 16, 2020

ACADEMIC

Explaining Italian "Exceptionalism" and Its End: Minimum Income From Neglect To Hyper‐Politicization

Matteo Jessoula, Marcello Natili | Social Policy & Administration

"Traditionally at the margins of the political debate, minimum income protection has recently become a key issue in Italian politics...socio‐political demand and political supply [can] explain the scope and direction of minimum income reforms...leading to the introduction and institutionalization of a MIS."
Common Knowledge? Business Intellectuals, Bric and the Production of Knowledge Across Global Finance and International Relations

Stephen Aris, Aglaya Snetkov | Journal of International Relations and Development

"...the science and technology studies' concept of boundary objects is invaluable in accounting for both how IR's 'constitutive' theorising is influenced by other circuits of practice and the co-constituted nature of 'the international' as an object of investigation...the functionality of BRIC as a boundary object served to provide business intellectuals with constitutive influence on how IR knows its object of study."
Leviathan As an Inventor: an Extended Agency Model of State-Owned Versus Private Firm Invention in Emerging and Developed Economies

Sergio G. Lazzarini, Luiz F. Mesquita, Felipe Monteiro, Aldo Musacchio | Journal of International Business Studies

"...from a traditional agency-theoretical standpoint, SOEs are plagued by low-powered incentives and dysfunctional involvement (e.g., wasteful budgets and political meddling), which reduces SOE performance...[However,] an extended agency model...entertains a more balanced view, in which these socalled agency liabilities in fact benefit SOEs...in some types of inventive output. ."
Cybernetics for the Command Economy: Foregrounding Entropy in Late Soviet Planning

Diana K. West | History of the Human Sciences

"Removed from Moscow bureaucracy and politics, the various planning institutes of the Siberian Academy of Sciences...put forth a vision of a dynamic Soviet economy managed through partially automated subsystems, which, while decentralized, would grant the central planning apparatus flexibility, a capacity for emergence, and overall solvency in the face of increasingly complex factors that required consideration."
New Liberalism and the City of London: Reassessing Empire, Finance, and Politics in Francis Hirst's Economist, 1906–16

Alexander Zevin | Twentieth Century British History

"[Francis Hirst's] tenure at The Economist-including his ejection in 1916 for supporting a negotiated peace during the First World War-...[reveals how] finance and politics were deeply intertwined in liberal understandings of free trade, empire, and social reform by the turn of the twentieth century...conflicts that emerged at this time, over the interests of the City [of London]...prefigured later, better-known clashes that have recurred in Britain down to the present."
Piero Sraffa's St. Simonian Temptations. An Examination of the Sraffa Papers

Michel Bellet, Adrien Lutz | The European Journal of the History of Economic Thought

"Why did Piero Sraffa (1898-1983), one of the most important economists of the 20th century, undertake such a significant-albeit never published-study of the St. Simonian texts?...In view of the particular characteristics of these unpublished works, the paper makes some interpretative proposals."
Workplace Biometrics: Protecting Employee Privacy One Fingerprint At a Time

Peter Holland, Tse L. Tham | Economic and Industrial Democracy

"[In a] major case study in Australia, a decision to terminate employment on the grounds of non-consent for biometric data to be collected was overturned through the legislative system...[highlighting] the increased impetus to collect such data...[and] the need [for employers] to understand employees' rights and their own obligations around the informational privacy of employees."

The Price of Peace: Money, Democracy, and the Life of John Maynard Keynes

Zachary D. Carter | Penguin Random House

"Keynes was not only an economist but the preeminent anti authoritarian thinker of the twentieth century, one who devoted his life to the belief that art and ideas could conquer war and deprivation."
Trade Wars Are Class Wars: How Rising Inequality Distorts the Global Economy and Threatens International Peace

Matthew C. Klein, Michael Pettis | Yale University Press

"This book traces the origins of today's trade wars to decisions made by politicians and business leaders in China, Europe, and the United States over the past thirty years."
Brazil's Revolution in Commerce: Creating Consumer Capitalism in the American Century

James P. Woodard | University of North Carolina Press

"This book tells how a new economic outlook took hold over the course of the twentieth century, a time when the United States became Brazil's most important trading partner and the tastemaker of its betterheeled citizens."
